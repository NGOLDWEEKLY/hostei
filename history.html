Disney Animations has now been 95 years!

Scroll down to see what Disney Animation had ever done over decades.

Scroll

1923

Kansas City, Missouri, natives Walt Disney and Roy O. Disney founded the Disney Brothers Cartoon Studio in Los Angeles and got their start producing a series of silent Alice Comedies short films featuring a live-action child actress in an animated world.

1926

January, the studio moved to 2719 Hyperion Avenue and took on the name the Walt Disney Studio.

1930

Disputes over finances between Disney and Powers led to Disney's studio, reincorporated on December 16, 1929, as Walt Disney Productions, signing a new distribution contract with Columbia Pictures.

1933

Technicolor Silly Symphony cartoon Three Little Pigs became a major box office and pop culture success, with its theme song "Who's Afraid of the Big Bad Wolf?" becoming a popular chart hit.

1934

Disney proceeded undaunted into the production of Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs, which would become the first animated feature in English and Technicolor.

1935

Mickey Mouse switched to Technicolor, by which time the series had added several major supporting characters, among them Mickey's dog Pluto and their friends Donald Duck and Goofy.

1940

The first of new animated features was Pinocchio, released in February.
Fantasia, an experimental film produced to an accompanying orchestral arrangement conducted by Leopold Stokowski, was released in November 1940 by Disney itself in a series of limited-seating roadshow engagements.

1948

Disney returned to the production of full-length features with Cinderella, a full-length film based on the fairy tale by Charles Perrault.

1951

Alice in Wonderland, met with a lukewarm response at the box office and was a sharp critical disappointment in its initial release.

1966

Walt Disney died in December, ten months before the studio's next film The Jungle Book, was completed and released.

1983

Lasseter, an animator, was fired from Disney, but went on to become the creative head of Pixar, a pioneering computer animation studio that would begin a close association with Disney in the late 1980s.

1988

Disney's started entering into Australia's long-standing animation industry, by purchasing Hanna-Barbera's Australian studio to start Disney Animation Australia.

1989

The Little Mermaid was the first to feature the use of Disney's Computer Animation Production System (CAPS) developed for Disney by Pixar, it would become significant in allowing future Disney films to more seamlessly integrate CGI and achieve higher production.

1992

Aladdin, continued the upward trend in Disney's animation success, earning $504 million worldwide at the box office, and two more Oscars for Best Song and Best Score.

1994

Disney president Frank Wells was killed in a helicopter accident, and Katzenberg lobbied CEO Michael Eisner for the vacant president position.

2001

Atlantis: The Lost Empire, an attempt to break the Disney formula by moving into action-adventure, received mixed reviews and earned $186 million worldwide against production costs of $120 million.

2005

Disney released its first fully computer-animated feature, Chicken Little. The film was a moderate success at the box office, earning $315 million worldwide, but was not well-received critically.

2013

the studio laid off nine of its hand-drawn animators, including Nik Ranieri and Ruben A. Aquino, leading to speculation on animation blogs that the studio was abandoning traditional animation, an idea that the studio dismissed.

2016

Moana, a CGI fantasy-adventure film, was released.

2018

Jennifer Lee was announced as Lasseter's replacement as chief creative officer of Disney Animation on June 19.
